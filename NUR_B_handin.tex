\subsection{(1b) }

We want to simulate the solar system 300 years in the future. For this, we will use the Leapfrog method as this is a symplectic integrator, which means that it approximately conserves the energy of a system, unlike methods such as Euler's method, RK4, etc. So, leapfrog is a good choice to simulate physical systems such as orbits. \textbf{EXPLAIN LEAPFROG} Leapfrog updates the position and velocity alternately. Before starting the leapfrog integrator, the velocity needs to be kicked by half a timestep. For this, we use RK4, which is explained further in 1c.
Both the orbits in the x-y plane, as well as the z position over time, are shown below. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{\PlotDir/orbits_xy_leapfrog.png}
    \caption{Orbits of the objects in the x-y plane using the leapfrog integrator.}
\end{figure}

The orbits from the simulations look to be correct and nicely closed. The only thing that stands out is that the orbit of Mercury seems to be less stable than the other orbits. This is probably due to Mercury being close to the Sun, so the forces acting on this planet are stronger, and the orbit is more strongly curved than, for example, the orbit of Neptune. To resolve this problem, you would need to take smaller time steps to make the result more accurate.
 
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{\PlotDir/z_vs_time_leapfrog.png}
    \caption{Position in the z plane as a function of time for all objects using the leapfrog integrator.}
\end{figure}

From the simulations, we also find that the z-position of the planets and the Sun, i.e., the height above the midplane, oscillates. This is expected as the planets follow elliptic orbits at an angle with the midplane, which results in these oscillations.
